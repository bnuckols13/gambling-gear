You Showed Up. That Matters.

Understanding a problem clearly, without judgment, is often where real change begins.

This group is built on two ideas: that you deserve honest information about what gambling does and why, and that you get to decide what you do with it. Nobody here will tell you who you are or what your future looks like. We're just going to look at some things together.

Today's session draws from the GEAR Program (the Gambling Evaluation and Reduction Program) and the Stages of Change model. You don't need to be at any particular stage for this to be useful. Come as you are.

Stages of Change

12 min

Change rarely happens all at once. It almost never follows a straight line. The Stages of Change model describes where people are in their relationship with a behavior. There's no "right" stage to be in. Each one is real and valid.

Select the stage that feels closest to where you are right now. You don't have to share it with anyone.

✓
Stage 1
Precontemplation
"I don't think gambling is really a problem for me."
✓
Stage 2
Contemplation
"Maybe it is a problem, but I'm not sure what to do about it."
✓
Stage 3
Preparation
"I know I need to do something. I'm getting ready to make a change."
✓
Stage 4
Action
"I'm actively making changes to my gambling behavior right now."
✓
Stage 5
Maintenance
"I've made changes and I'm working to keep them going."

If You're in Precontemplation

You're here, which means some part of you is asking questions. You don't need to be convinced of anything today. Just notice what comes up as you listen. Information without pressure is often what shifts things.

Most people move through this stage when they start seeing the gap between what gambling promised and what it actually delivered.

If You're in Contemplation

Ambivalence is not weakness. It's honest. Gambling has given you something (excitement, escape, connection, control) and it has cost you something. Both are real. Today isn't about deciding which side wins. It's about seeing both more clearly.

This stage can feel like being stuck. That's normal. Movement usually comes from getting more specific about the costs, not from being pushed.

If You're in Preparation

You've decided something needs to change. The main work now is getting concrete: what specifically will you do differently, and when? Vague intentions ("I'll cut back") are less effective than specific plans ("I won't go to the casino on Fridays").

This group can help you identify what situations and feelings to plan around.

If You're in Action

You're doing the hardest thing. Action is exhausting because it requires sustained effort against old habits and familiar urges. The most important thing to know: relapse is part of the process, not evidence you've failed.

Understanding your triggers, specifically, is what makes action stick over time.

If You're in Maintenance

You've done something real. Maintenance is its own stage, not just "not relapsing." It's building a life where the pull of gambling has less and less to offer. Understanding what originally drove the behavior helps protect against complacency.

Sharing what's worked is one of the most valuable things you can offer this group today.

Important to Know

Change is rarely linear. Most people move forward, then backward, then forward again. Returning to an earlier stage is not failure. It's a normal part of the process. Getting back to the work is what matters, not the path it takes.

You can invite brief sharing: "Would anyone be comfortable sharing roughly where they are?" Keep it optional. The goal is normalization, not disclosure. Emphasize that all stages are welcome, and that Precontemplation in the room means someone brave showed up while still uncertain.

Understanding the Problem: GEAR

18 min

The GEAR program begins with one foundational idea: you can't change what you can't see clearly. This section is about building that picture, not to judge it, but to understand it well enough to work with it.

Problem gambling isn't about being weak or irresponsible. It's a learned behavior that gets reinforced by the brain in powerful ways. Understanding the mechanism is the first step to loosening its hold.

🔁

The Gambling Cycle

Gambling behavior tends to run in a predictable cycle. Seeing it written out often makes people feel less alone. The cycle is not personal. It's a pattern.

Phase 1
Before
Urges, fantasies, "hunches." Thoughts about winning. A feeling that this time will be different.
→
Phase 2
During
Seeking access. Finding money. Removing barriers. Chasing losses to "get back."
→
Phase 3
After
Financial loss, broken promises, guilt, shame, depression, which often feed back into Phase 1.
The Hook

The brain releases dopamine during gambling, not just on wins, but on near-misses and unpredictable rewards. This intermittent reinforcement is more addictive than consistent rewards. The machine is designed to feel like you're almost winning.

cognitive distortions
🧠

What Your Brain Tells You

Gambling thrives on specific thinking errors, patterns of thought that feel true but don't hold up. Recognizing them doesn't make them disappear immediately, but it creates a small gap between the thought and the action.

🎰

Gambler's Fallacy

"I've lost five times in a row. A win has to be due." The truth: each bet is completely independent. Past results don't influence future ones.
🎯

Illusion of Control

"If I touch the machine a certain way, or sit in this seat, I have better odds." The truth: skill and ritual cannot change random outcomes.
💡

Near-Miss Bias

"I almost won. I'm getting closer. One more and I'll have it." The truth: near-misses are designed into games to feel like progress. They aren't.
📊

Selective Memory

"I'm actually pretty good at this. I win a lot." The truth: wins are remembered vividly. Losses, which are larger, are minimized or forgotten.
📈

Chasing Losses

"If I keep going, I'll win it back. I just need to recover what I lost." The truth: chasing losses is the single most predictable way to make them worse.
🔮

Predictive Illusion

"I have a good feeling about this. I can tell today is different." The truth: hunches and feelings do not predict outcomes in games of chance.

Triggers & High-Risk Situations

A trigger is any thought, feeling, person, place, or situation that increases the urge to gamble. Identifying yours, specifically, is one of the most practical things you can do in early recovery.

Emotional Triggers

  • Stress, anxiety, or feeling overwhelmed
  • Boredom, gambling as stimulation or escape
  • Anger, frustration, feeling out of control
  • Depression, loneliness, emptiness
  • Guilt or shame, often about gambling itself

Environmental Triggers

  • Driving past a casino or betting shop
  • Being around others who gamble
  • Access to cash or credit
  • Seeing gambling ads or apps
  • Specific times of day, week, or year

Behavioral Triggers

  • Having a "small win" that reactivates the cycle
  • Idle time with no structure or plans
  • Periods of financial stress or unexpected money
  • Feeling like you've "earned a break" after progress

Group Discussion

20 min
◇ Everything here is an invitation. Pass as many times as you need.
  • Opening Round

    Did anything in today's content land differently than you expected: something that surprised you, made you uncomfortable, or felt familiar?

  • The Cycle

    Looking at the three-phase gambling cycle. Which phase do you find hardest to interrupt? Where does it feel most "sticky" for you?

  • Recognizing Distortions

    Which cognitive distortion showed up most in your own gambling? What does it sound like in your head? What does it feel like to name it?

  • Triggers

    What's one trigger you've identified that you'd be willing to share? What does it usually lead to if you don't catch it?

  • What's Possible

    If you imagine yourself in a different relationship with gambling, even vaguely, what does that look like? What would be different?

Watch for shame spirals in this discussion: someone starting to self-attack. Gently redirect: "I want to slow down here. Can we look at this with a little more curiosity and a little less judgment?" The goal is insight, not confession.

If someone is still in Precontemplation, affirm their presence: "The fact that you're in this room while still unsure is actually significant." Do not push.
